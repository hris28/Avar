

The rain falls without sound.

Or perhaps it makes sound, and the weight of grief simply swallows it. Either way, the plaza before the Duke's estate is filled with people draped in mourning cloth, and all you hear is the occasional shuffle of feet on wet stone, the clearing of throats, the suppressed sounds of those trying not to weep in public.

The year is 618. **The [[Duke]] of Zen'tar is dead.**

The official proclamation said it was his heart. A sudden failure. Peacefully, in his sleep. The physicians confirmed it. The priests blessed the body. The records were sealed with appropriate ceremony.

No one **believes it**.

But no one says otherwise. Not here. Not now. Not when the plaza is filled with [[eye]]s that watch and ears that listen and mouths that report back to powers that prefer convenient [[truth]]s to dangerous questions.

The casket sits on a raised platform draped in the city's colors: deep blue and silver, the crest of [[Zen'tar]] emblazoned on cloth that ripples in the wind. It's closed. No viewing. The Duke's face, they said, should be remembered as it was in life—strong, certain, commanding.
Around the plaza's edges, the vultures circle.

Not literal ones, though the metaphor fits. The merchant guild representatives stand in careful clusters, already negotiating who will fill the void in trade authority. The noble houses position themselves with strategic spacing, each calculating their proximity to power. The temple delegates murmur prayers that sound more like political speeches than devotions.

And in the background, barely visible through the crowd, a figure moves with practiced ease. High elf ([[Aeilin (High Elves)]]). Elegant robes. Speaking to one person, then another, then a third. Always listening more than talking. Always positioned where information flows.

The political machine is already turning. The Duke's body isn't even cold, and the struggle for succession has begun.

--
The young [[prince]] steps forward. He can't be more than fifteen. Sixteen at most. His face is composed, but his hands shake as he unfolds a piece of parchment. He reads:

"My father was a man of principle. He believed in justice. In truth. In the idea that Zen'tar could be more than the sum of its factions and families. He believed we could be better."

His voice cracks slightly. He swallows, continues.

"He taught me that leadership is not about power. It's about service. About standing between those you protect and whatever darkness threatens them. Even when the cost is everything."

A pause. His eyes drift to the casket, then away.

"I will honor his memory. I will continue his work. I will—"

He stops. Looks down at the parchment as if seeing words there he didn't write. Words he's expected to say but doesn't believe.
"I will do my best."

He folds the paper. Steps back. Disappears into the crowd of family and advisors who immediately close around him like a protective, or controlling, wall.

The high elf in the background shifts position. Closer to the prince's retinue. Listening.

**Statement**
No one announces him. He simply steps forward from the crowd, and people make space without quite knowing why. He's dressed in simple mourning clothes: dark grey, no ornamentation, a hood pulled low enough to shadow his face. Just another mourner. Just another citizen paying respects.

But when he speaks, his voice carries. Not loud. Just… certain.

"I knew the Duke before he was the Duke."

Heads turn. People lean in. This wasn't on the program.

"We adventured together. Years ago. When Zen'tar was smaller and the world was larger. When the only titles that mattered were the ones you earned with blood and sweat and trust."

He looks at the casket. Not with the distant respect of a citizen, but with the familiar grief of someone who's lost a brother.
"He was the smart one. Always. The one who saw three moves ahead while the rest of us were still figuring out the first. He kept us alive more times than I can count. Not with a sword, he was terrible with a sword, but with his mind. With his ability to see patterns where others saw chaos."
A faint smile, bitter and fond.

"We used to argue. Constantly. About tactics. About risks. About whether the smart play was always the right play. He believed in calculated moves. I believed in necessary ones. We were both wrong. We were both right. That's what made us good together."|

He shifts, and for a moment, the hood slips enough to show the edge of a scar, the set of a jaw, the shadow of someone who once wore armor instead of merchant cloth.

"The last time we spoke, he told me he'd found something. Something that changed everything he thought he knew about this city. About what we're built on. About what we're protecting."

The crowd is silent now. Even the vultures have stopped circling.
"He said it was beautiful. Terrible. Bright. Inevitable."
The man looks up at the casket, and his voice drops to something quieter but no less clear.

"He said he was going to try to break a pattern. Even knowing it would cost him. Because that's who he was. He saw the cost. He paid it anyway."
A pause. The rain continues to fall without sound.

"They'll tell you he died peacefully. In his sleep. Of natural causes."

Another pause, heavier than the last.

"Believe what you need to believe. But know this: my friend died doing what he always did. Standing between this city and a darkness most of you don't even know exists. And he bought you time. Years. Maybe Decades. Maybe just Months."
He pulls the hood lower, preparing to fade back into the crowd.

"Honor him. Not with words. With action. With the courage to ask the questions he asked. With the willingness to pay the prices he paid."
"And if you can't do that, then at least don't waste what he bought you."

He turns to go, then stops. Looks back at the casket one last time.

"Goodbye, old friend. I'll keep the [[hourglass]] turning. I promise."

And then he's gone, disappearing into the crowd like smoke, leaving behind a silence heavier than grief.

**Post Ceremony**
The crowd disperses slowly. The prince is escorted away by his handlers. The merchant guilds begin their negotiations in earnest. The noble houses position their pieces.

And in a shadowed alcove near the plaza's edge, two figures meet.

The man has removed his hood now that he's away from the main crowd. The rain drips from his hair, but he doesn't seem to notice. Standing beside him, silent and still as carved stone, is [[Valerian Than'dal]]. The Knight. Armor concealed beneath a long cloak, but unmistakable in bearing.
The man speaks first, voice low and urgent.

"Did you see them? The high elf. Third cluster from the prince. Talking to the merchant council representative, the temple delegate, and someone I didn't recognize."

Valerian nods once. His voice is quiet, edged with something that might be anger or might be grief. "[[House Maelis]]. They've been circling since before the body was cold. They want the succession contested. Chaos serves their interests. Then there is [[House Bluefist]]...already they call for the general's return to restore order. Not even mentioning [[Morren]]."

"Of course it does." The man wipes rain from his face. "How long do you think we have before they make their move?"
"Weeks. A month at most. The [[prince]] is young. Unprepared. He'll be pressured to align with one faction or another. Once he does, the others will push back. The city will [[fracture]]."

The man curses softly. "He wasn't supposed to die yet. We needed more time. More pieces in place. The ledger was supposed to be leverage, not a death sentence."

Valerian's jaw tightens. "He knew the risk. He took it anyway."

"I know. I know." The man runs a hand through his wet hair, frustration and grief warring in his expression. "But damn him for being noble about it. Damn him for thinking [[truth]] mattered more than timing."

A pause. The rain falls. Somewhere in the distance, the crowd's murmur fades.

The man turns to face Valerian fully.

"The timeline is accelerated now. We no longer have the luxury of patience and maneuver. Whatever we were planning to do in decades, we do in year. Whatever we were saving for the right moment, we spend now."

Valerian meets his gaze. "The prince?"

"Protect him if you can. But don't prioritize him over the mission. The Duke died protecting what he found. We don't let that be for nothing."
"And if the factions move against each other? If the city tears itself apart?"

The man's expression hardens into something cold and calculating. The face of a man who's made terrible decisions before and will make them again.

"Then we use the chaos. We move while they're distracted. We find what the Duke found. We finish what he started."

He looks back toward where the casket sits, now being prepared for transport to the family crypt.

"He bought us time with his life. I won't waste it being careful."

Valerian is silent for a long moment. Then: "What do you need me to do?"

"Watch the prince. From a distance. You are my hands. Make sure he survives the next week. After that..." The man's hand goes to something beneath his cloak, a pendant, shaped like an [[hourglass]], handing it to the knight. "I need to have a conversation with one of my other old friends and bring him into the fold. After that, we'll see what pieces are still on the board. "

"And the item? The one the Duke found?"

"Gone. Either they took it when they killed him, or he hid it somewhere even I can't find. We'll have to work without it."
[[Midas]] pulls his hood back up, becoming just another mourner again.

"We meet in three days. Usual place. Bring whatever intelligence you can gather on who's moving where. And Valerian?"

The Knight looks at him.

"We're running out of time. Out of allies. Out of grains in the hourglass. Whatever we do next, it has to count. It has to matter. Because we won't get another chance."

Valerian nods once. "Understood."

They separate, melting back into different parts of the dispersing crowd. Two pieces on a board that's gotten infinitely more complicated. Two players in a game that just lost one of its most important pieces.

Behind them, the Duke's casket is lifted by the honor guard.

And in the background, barely visible through the rain, the high elf continues their quiet conversations, gathering information, building networks, positioning pieces.

The game continues.
The hourglass turns.

And somewhere in the deep places beneath [[Zen'tar]], something that has been patient for hundreds of years stirs slightly, as if sensing that the rhythm above has changed.

That the count is accelerating.
That the time for watching may soon be ending.
