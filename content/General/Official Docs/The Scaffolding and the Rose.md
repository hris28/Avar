
They tell you, when you're young and foolish enough to believe in things like justice, that the world is made of clear lines. Good on one side, evil on the other. Heroes and villains. Right and wrong.

[[Crane]] taught him differently.

"The world," his mentor said, perched on a rooftop overlooking [[Zen'tar]]'s second circle, "is made of compromise. Of necessary evils. Of people trying to do the least harm while achieving the most good. And sometimes, boy, those two things are the same knife, just held by different hands."
The boy, he had a name, but names were currency in their line of work, spent carefully and never on introductions, listened. He always listened when Crane spoke. Not because Crane demanded it, but because every word felt like a key to a lock he hadn't known existed.

"We're not heroes," Crane continued, eyes tracking movement in the street below. "The Order doesn't deal in heroism. We deal in necessity. In the actions that keep the city standing even when the city doesn't want to know what's holding it up."

"Like scaffolding," the boy offered.

Crane smiled. It was rare. The boy treasured it.
"Exactly like scaffolding. Hidden. Ugly. Doing the work no one thanks you for."

The boy shifted his weight, careful not to disturb the loose tiles. "And if the scaffolding breaks?"

"Then the building falls. And everyone inside wonders why no one saw it coming." Crane stood, brushing dust from his dark coat. "Come on. We have a meeting."

That was three years ago.
## Mission
The mission was supposed to be simple.

Reconnaissance only, the rose-sealed orders read. 
_Observe the Blue Sail main warehouse complex, primary wharf, lower harbor. Document movement patterns related to recent material acquisitions. Identify key personnel involved in procurement chain. Do not engage. Report by dawn._

Simple. Clean. The kind of work the boy had done a dozen times under Crane's guidance.

Except this time felt different.
Crane was tense. More than usual. He'd spent three days reviewing maps, contacts, exit routes; the kind of preparation you did when you expected things to go wrong.
"This is their main operation," the boy said, studying the wharf from their vantage point two buildings over. "Not a satellite. We're looking at their headquarters."

"Yes."
"That's bold. Even for us."

"Not bold. Necessary." Crane's jaw tightened. "The materials they're moving aren't normal contraband. Awakened metals. Pre-cataclysm artifacts. Things that shouldn't exist in these quantities. Someone is supplying them, and Mithris is actively managing it."

The boy frowned, counting guards. Sixteen visible. Probably twice that inside. "And we're just observing?"

"We're confirming a pattern. Once we know what's moving and when, the Order can make informed decisions about intervention."

"Maybe they're expanding?"

"Maybe." Crane didn't sound convinced. "Or maybe someone wants us looking in the wrong place. Or the right place for the wrong reasons."

The boy studied his mentor's profile. Three years of working together had taught him to read the subtle tells. The way Crane's hand kept drifting toward his knife. The way his eyes tracked not just guards but shadows.

"Do we abort?"
"No. We need this intelligence. But we do it carefully. In, observe, out. No risks."

Famous last words, the boy would think later. The kind of thing people said right before everything went wrong.
## The Quiet Before the Storm
They moved like smoke through the warehouse district. Years of training made the boy's steps silent, his breathing controlled.

Crane led them to a position overlooking the main wharf entrance. From here, they could see the central courtyard, the primary warehouse, and the smaller buildings that served as barracks and storage.

"There." Crane pointed. A young man, younger than the boy himself, stood near a side entrance. Not guarding. Positioned. Ready.

"That one's different," the boy murmured.

"[[Caldun]]. The [[Blue Pirates]]' enforcer. Trained from childhood. He doesn't question orders. Doesn't hesitate. Doesn't fail."

The boy watched how the other guards gave Caldun space. How they moved around him like water around a stone. "He's m age."

"He's a weapon that happens to be shaped like a person. There's a difference."

Movement. The side door opened. A figure emerged - tall, commanding, dressed in the fine coat of someone who'd risen from the streets to something resembling legitimacy. [[Mithris]] himself. The Blue Pirates' leader rarely appeared in person at operations like this.

"That's unusual," Crane whispered. "Mithris doesn't risk exposure for routine shipments."

Mithris handed something to Caldun. A package. Small. Wrapped in oilcloth, but even from this distance, the boy could see it was heavy. The way Caldun's hand dipped slightly when he took it. The careful way he cradled it.

Caldun nodded once and turned toward the warehouse interior.
And then the night shattered.

**War**

The attack didn't come from the harbor. It came from the front.

One man. Walking through the main gate like he owned it. Like he'd been invited.

The boy saw him clearly in the lamplight: young, maybe mid-twenties, wearing the formal coat of nobility but moving like a trained fighter. Sword already drawn. Face set with the kind of cold fury that came from decisions made long before tonight.

"That's [[Arthur]] Augustus," Crane breathed, recognition sharp in his voice."He is the Regent Council Representative and Senator's son with the investigator's detachment. What is he -"

Arthurian didn't announce himself. Didn't give warning.
He just started killing.
The first guard died before raising an alarm. The second managed half a shout before Arthurian's blade took his throat. By the third, the wharf was scrambling, Blue Pirates pouring from buildings, weapons drawn.
And Arthurian cut through them like they were wheat.

"One man," the boy whispered, unable to look away. "He's fighting them alone."

"Not fighting. Executing." Crane's voice was tight. "That's not tactics. That's rage. That's someone who's made this personal."

The boy watched Arthurian move. Every strike precise but brutal. Efficient but excessive. The kind of violence that came from hatred barely leashed.
Magic flared around him; not complex workings, just raw combat enhancement. Speed. Strength. Endurance. The kind of expensive enchantments only noble houses could afford.

But it was the way he fought that made the boy's breath catch. Like he didn't care if he lived. Like survival was secondary to making sure every Blue Pirate who stood against him died first.

Mithris emerged from the warehouse, bellowing orders, trying to rally his forces. The Blue Pirates were numerous, experienced, well-armed. They should have overwhelmed one man through sheer numbers.

But Arthurian didn't give them the chance to coordinate. He pushed forward, always forward, creating chaos, forcing them to react instead of plan.

And in the middle of it all, Caldun appeared.
The two locked eyes across twenty feet of carnage.
Then Caldun moved.
## Fin
"We need to leave," Crane hissed, already pulling back. "This isn't our concern. We extract now before - "

The explosion came from inside the warehouse. Where Caldun had been heading with the package.

_A wave of wrongness that rippled outward, making the air shimmer and bend, making reality itself seem to stutter and crack._ 

Awakened material. Triggered either deliberately or by the chaos outside.
The boy felt it hit like a fist to the chest. His vision swam. His ears rang. And when he could see clearly again, the wharf was worse than a war zone.
It was a nightmare.

The Blue Pirates fighting Arthurian. Arthurian carving through them with methodical savagery. And now, reality itself fracturing at the edges, pieces of the warehouse bending at wrong angles, shadows pointing directions they shouldn't.

Caldun emerged from the warehouse, blades drawn but moving differently now. Injured. Slower. The awakening had caught him too.
But he saw Arthurian. Saw the threat.
And moved to intercept.
The two collided with the sound of steel on steel. Caldun was trained, deadly, precise. But Arthurian fought with something beyond training. Something that made the boy's stomach turn.
**Conviction**.
The kind that said: I will die here if I must, but you die first.

"That's not a raid," Crane breathed. "That's a vendetta."
"Against Mithris?"
"Hell if I know! Against all of them." Crane grabbed the boy's arm. "We leave. Now. Before -"

Caldun spotted them.
Not Arthurian. Not the remaining Blue Pirates. Them.
The boy saw recognition in those cold eyes. Saw calculation. Saw decision.
Caldun broke from Arthurian, moving toward their position with terrifying speed.
And Crane was already pushing the boy back, creating space, drawing attention.
"Run."

"I'm not leaving you!"
"That's an order." Crane's voice carried absolute authority. "This isn't about the mission anymore. Run. Get to the safehouse. Report everything. The attack. Augustus. The awakened material. The package. Tell them the harbor is compromised and a noble is tearing it apart."

"Crane"

"Run."

The boy ran.
He would regret it for the rest of his life.
But he ran.
## Aftermath
He made it four blocks before he stopped, pressed against a wall in an alley that reeked of salt and blood, chest heaving, mind screaming at him to go back. But Crane's training held. When your mentor gives you an order to retreat, you retreat. Because if both of you die, the mission dies with you.
So he waited. Counted to a thousand. Heard the sounds of combat fade in the distance fade.
He started back.

The boy found Crane in the space between two warehouses, propped against a wall, blood spreading across his chest in a pattern too wide to survive.
Caldun was gone. The scene was empty except for the dying man and the boy who'd failed him.

"You came back." Crane's voice was wet. Wrong. "Should've... kept running."
The boy dropped to his knees, hands hovering uselessly over wounds he couldn't fix. "I can get help. I can - "
"No time." Crane coughed. Crimson on his lips. "Listen. The package. Caldun had it when the awakening triggered. That means... someone wanted that material there. At that exact time. Either to destroy it or use the chaos as cover."

"Forget the package -"
"Listen." Crane gripped his arm with surprising strength. "Augustus. He's probably dead or dying by now. No one survives that kind of assault alone. But he'll have done damage. Massive damage. The Blue Pirates will weaken. And whoever supplied that awakened material..."

"What does it mean?"

"Pattern. Always a pattern. Augustus attacks for other reasons. Awakened material triggers during the chaos. We're there to observe. Too many pieces moving at once." He coughed, weaker. "Someone orchestrated this. Maybe not all of it. But... parts."|

The boy felt tears on his face and didn't care. "What do I do?"

"Report. Investigate. Find out what the package really was and why it mattered enough to trigger during an assassination attempt." Crane's grip tightened one last time. "And remember: we're not heroes. We're scaffolding. Don't let anyone make you feel guilty for doing what's necessary."

"Crane"

"Your name. Your real name."
Names were currency. But this felt like a gift Crane was asking for.

"[[Artemis]]," he whispered.

Crane smiled faintly. "Good name. Hunter. Protector." He coughed. "You'll grow into it."
His hand slipped from Artemis's arm.
His chest stopped rising.

And Artemis sat there, holding the body of the man who'd taught him the world was made of compromise and necessary evil.
Sat there until the cold settled in.
Until the grief hardened into something else.
## Eight Years Later...
Artemis was twenty-five when he learned what had really happened that night.

Arthurian Augustus had survived. Barely. The noble had killed Mithris in single combat, but the cost had been catastrophic. Crippled. Left barely able to walk. His fighting days ended in one night of fury and vengeance. But he'd won. The Blue Pirates had fractured, a shell of their former self. The harbor's underworld had collapsed into infighting and chaos.

And Arthurian had returned to the Senate, to the Investigator's detachment, carrying the scars of what he'd done like badges of honor and shame in equal measure.

Artemis had never approached him. Never asked why. Never revealed that he'd been there that night, watching from the shadows.
Because he understood, by then, what Crane had tried to teach him.
The world wasn't made of heroes and villains.
It was made of people doing what they thought was necessary.

Arthurian, burning with fury, tearing apart corruption with his bare hands.
Caldun, serving the only purpose he'd been taught.
Crane, dying to buy intelligence that might save lives later.
Himself, learning to live with the weight of survival when others had died.
**
All of them scaffolding.
All of them holding up a building that didn't know their names.
All of them breaking, eventually, under the weight.**

The package had never been recovered. The material had disappeared. The supplier had gone to ground.

But Artemis had kept searching.
Following threads. Building patterns. Asking questions about materials that shouldn't exist and the people who moved them.
And eventually, those questions would lead him to something else. Something older. Something that would make everything that happened that night look like a prelude.

**But that was still to come.**

For now, standing in the safehouse, reading reports about Senator Augustus's anti-corruption initiatives and the permanent limp he carried, Artemis understood something Crane had died trying to teach him.

The world was made of compromise.
Of necessary evils.
Of people trying to do the least harm while achieving the most good.
And sometimes, those two things were the same knife, just held by different hands.
